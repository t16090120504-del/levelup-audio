## Series Overview

Alex Reyes works at Meridian Technologies, a seemingly normal tech company that secretly develops tools for monitoring, containing, and erasing supernatural incidents from the public eye. Alex is a "Cleaner" — the field operative who handles the messy reality of magic bleeding into the mundane world. When entities that shouldn't exist according to any known magical taxonomy start appearing across the city, Alex's investigation leads him to suspect someone inside Meridian is creating them. His only ally is Dr. Sarah Chen, the company's brilliant, reckless lead researcher who has her own reasons for wanting the truth. Together they navigate corporate espionage, bureaucratic cover-ups, and the growing realization that the supernatural world is far more fragile than anyone believed.

## Episode 1: "The Cleanup"

[narration begins]

Rule one of supernatural cleanup: never touch the blood.

Not because it's dangerous — though it usually is — but because blood is evidence, and evidence means paperwork. And paperwork means explaining to Meridian's legal department why a Level 4 Ghoul Manifestation required thirty-seven rounds of blessed ammunition and a new drywall installation at a Chipotle in downtown Seattle.

My name is Alex Reyes. I'm a Cleaner for Meridian Technologies, which is a fancy way of saying I'm the guy who shows up after something magical goes wrong and makes it look like nothing happened. Gas leak. Electrical fire. Spontaneous structural failure. The standard cover stories never change because the public never wants to believe the truth.

I crouched behind the Chipotle's service counter, scanning the restaurant through the heads-up display built into my glasses. Meridian tech — standard issue for field operatives. Thermal imaging, spectral analysis, and a very helpful AI assistant named ORACLE that whispered tactical data directly into my ear canal.

**ORACLE: Hostile neutralized. Spectral residue at 12%. Recommend containment protocol Gamma-7.**

"Already on it," I muttered, pulling a containment canister from my belt. The canister was about the size of a thermos and designed to vacuum up residual magical energy like a paranormal Roomba. I sprayed the ghoul's remains — what was left of them, anyway — and watched the blue-white mist get sucked into the canister's crystalline matrix.

Unknown. That word meant something in my line of work. After two hundred years of Meridian cataloguing every magical symbol, entity type, and spell form on Earth, "unknown" was supposed to be impossible.

Normal job. Normal company. Normal city.

## Episode 2: "The Researcher"

Sitting across from me was Dr. Sarah Chen. Meridian's lead researcher. Thirty-one, borderline-insomnia chic, with dark eyes that suggested she hadn't slept since the Clinton administration and a lab coat that had more coffee stains than fabric.

## Episode 5: "The Director"
**Duration target: ~4 minutes (~600 words)**

## Episode 6: "The Nameless"
**Duration target: ~4 minutes (~600 words)**

The containment chamber shattered.

The sphere of darkness didn't fly out — it expanded. Like a drop of ink in water, it spread across the walls, the ceiling, the floor, consuming the blue-white symbols Hargrove had painted. Where the darkness touched, the symbols went dark. Silenced.

I grabbed Sarah and pulled her toward the door. Hargrove stumbled backward, his composure finally shattered.

"What is that thing?" I shouted.

"I call it the Nameless," Hargrove said, his voice shaking. "It appeared in my research three months ago. I didn't summon it. I didn't create it. It was... waiting. In the gaps between magical systems. In the spaces where reality doesn't have rules."

"It's an Old Power?"

"No. It's something else. Something that predates the Old Powers. Something that existed before there were rules for reality to follow."

[beat]

The darkness spread faster now. It reached the door and flowed around it — not stopped, not blocked, simply... ignoring the barrier. The darkness didn't break physical objects. It passed through them.

My weapon was useless. I fired a blessed silver round into the spreading dark. The bullet passed through it and embedded in the far wall. The darkness continued.

Sarah activated her disruptor. The device flared — pale light cutting into the darkness — and for a moment it worked. The spreading black mass recoiled, pulling back like a burned hand. But then it adapted. The darkness shifted, thickened, and absorbed the disruptor's energy like a sponge.

"Impressive technology," a voice said from everywhere and nowhere. Not Hargrove's voice. Not human. "But you cannot disrupt what was never constructed."

The Nameless spoke. The sound vibrated in my chest, not through my ears but through my bones. It was a sound that shouldn't exist — a frequency that reality itself wanted to reject.

"You've been feeding it," Sarah said to Hargrove, horror dawning. "Your synthesis matrix. The fractal symbols. You were channeling energy into it every time you created an entity."

"The symbols were supposed to be a containment framework," Hargrove whispered. "A way to bind the Nameless into a usable form. I thought I was in control."

"You were never in control. It let you think you were. It used your research as a conduit."

[beat]

The darkness paused. The voice came again, curious, almost gentle.

"Your fear is delicious. But unnecessary. I do not wish to destroy. I wish to understand."

"Understand what?" I asked, my weapon still raised despite its uselessness.

"Shape. Form. Limitation. I am without these things. I have watched your kind build structures of stone and law and magic for millennia. I wish to experience what it means to be... contained."

The darkness coalesced. In the center of the room, it formed a shape — human, roughly, but wrong. Too tall. Too thin. Features that shifted like smoke. Two eyes that held no color, no light, no depth.

"You are building a body," Sarah breathed. "It's using your research to build itself a physical form."

"Not a body," the Nameless corrected. "A suit. Something to wear. Something that fits within your rules so I might walk among you without breaking them."

It looked at me. At Sarah. At Hargrove.

"Which of you will teach me to be small?"

[end episode]

## Episode 7: "Stakeout"
**Duration target: ~4 minutes (~600 words)**

We got out of Sub-Level 5 alive. Barely.

The Nameless let us leave. I don't know why — maybe because we were more useful alive, or maybe because its human suit wasn't finished yet and we weren't worth the effort. Either way, we climbed thirty-eight floors of emergency stairs in silence, emerged into the predawn rain of Seattle, and sat in my car for ten minutes without speaking.

Sarah broke first. "Hargrove was right about one thing. Meridian can't know about this."

"An Old Power-level entity is loose in Seattle and Meridian can't know?"

"Meridian's response to an existential threat is containment. They'd seal the city, evacuate the population, and glass the whole area with orbital thaumic weapons. Millions dead. And it still might not work against something that predates reality's rules."

"So what do we do?"

"We do what Cleaners do. We investigate. We find a weakness. And we contain it ourselves, off the books."

[beat]

The next two weeks changed everything. Not because of the Nameless — it went quiet after Bay 11, retreating into whatever space between spaces it occupied. The change was us.

Sarah and I spent every waking hour together. Stakeouts. Research sessions. Late-night drives through empty streets, bouncing theories off each other while Seattle slept. We ate terrible diner food at 3 AM. We argued about magical theory, containment protocols, and whether my field methodology or her laboratory analysis was more valuable.

We learned each other's rhythms. She talked faster when she was excited, pacing like a caged animal, hands cutting through the air. I went quiet when I was thinking, staring at nothing, processing data in silence. She drank coffee like it was a medical necessity. I survived on whatever the convenience store had.

She was brilliant. Infuriating. Funny in a dry, acerbic way that caught me off guard. And she was scared — not of the Nameless, but of failure. Of not being smart enough. Of watching people die because her research wasn't fast enough.

"The world is breaking, Reyes," she said one night, sitting on the hood of my car outside an abandoned warehouse we'd been monitoring. "And everyone I work with is either too blind to see it or too scared to act. You're the first person who showed up when I called."

"I showed up because it's my job."

"No. You showed up because you're not an idiot. And because underneath all that Cleaner cynicism, you actually care."

I didn't have a response to that. So I handed her my coffee and didn't say anything, and she smiled — small, real, barely visible in the streetlight — and that was the moment I knew this was becoming more than professional.

[beat]

On the fourteenth day, ORACLE detected a new symbol. Not at an incident site — at an address in Capitol Hill. A residential apartment. Registered to a Meridian employee.

Dr. Sarah Chen.

## Episode 8: "The Mark"
**Duration target: ~4 minutes (~600 words)**

"You want to explain why your apartment is radiating an unregistered thaumic signature?"

"I don't know. I thought it was a bruise. It appeared after Bay 11, but I assumed—"

"No. But I can promise I won't stop trying."

## Episode 9: "The Convergence"
**Duration target: ~4 minutes (~600 words)**

The Nameless manifested three days later.

Not in a back alley. Not in an abandoned warehouse. In the middle of Pike Place Market at noon on a Saturday. Thousands of people. Cameras everywhere. The worst possible scenario.

I got the call from ORACLE while I was sleeping — the AI's voice cutting through four hours of unconsciousness like a knife.

**ORACLE: Critical alert. Thaumic event detected at Pike Place Market. Classification: Unknown entity. Estimated power level: Catastrophic. All Meridian operatives recalled to response stations.**

I was out the door in ninety seconds.

Sarah was faster. She'd been at Meridian Tower all night, running simulations on the Nameless's pattern. She arrived at the market before me and texted one word: "RUN."

I didn't run. I'm a Cleaner. Running isn't in the job description.

[beat]

Pike Place was chaos. Not the normal weekend chaos of tourists and fish throwers — this was supernatural chaos. Reality bent at the edges. Streetlights flickered between colors that don't have names. Shadows moved independently of the objects casting them. And in the center of the market, standing in front of the famous fish stall, was the Nameless.

Its suit was better now. More refined. It looked almost human — tall, thin, dressed in a dark coat that seemed to absorb the surrounding light. Its face was smooth and featureless, like a mannequin, but its movements were natural. Fluid. Practiced.

It was learning to be small.

People were fleeing, screaming, recording on phones that would show nothing once Meridian's cover-up protocols activated. But the damage was done — the Nameless had announced its presence to the world, and no amount of gas leak explanations would erase the memories of thousands of witnesses.

"Reyes!" Sarah sprinted toward me through the crowd, her tablet clutched to her chest. The mark on her arm was blazing — blue light visible through her sleeve. "The convergence point. It's anchored here — Pike Place is a natural thaumic nexus. The market was built on one of the city's oldest ley line intersections."

"Ley lines are theoretical."

"They're not theoretical anymore. The Nameless is using the ley line to stabilize its manifestation. If we don't disrupt it in the next twenty minutes, the suit becomes permanent. It becomes real."

[beat]

I approached the Nameless. It turned its featureless face toward me and I felt the weight of its attention — not hostile, but vast. Like staring into the ocean.

"Cleaner," it said. The word sounded like it was tasting something. "Your function is to erase. To make things as if they never were."

"That's the job."

"And if I asked you not to erase me? If I asked you to let me exist?"

"You're threatening the lives of everyone in this city."

"I am learning to exist within your rules. I have caused no deaths. I have harmed no one. I simply... appeared."

"You broke reality in a public market. That's harm."

The Nameless tilted its head — an eerily human gesture. "You are afraid of what you don't understand. She is not." It looked past me, at Sarah. "She understands. She feels me. The connection we share."

Sarah's arm was burning. I could see it now — the mark spreading, branching, growing like roots across her skin. She was becoming the bridge. And the Nameless was walking across it.

## Episode 10: "Protocol Midnight"
**Duration target: ~5 minutes (~750 words)**

The Nameless reached for Sarah.

I moved without thinking — twenty years of field training, every combat course Meridian ever forced me through, compressed into a single action. I intercepted the Nameless's hand with a blessed iron strike that should have severed it at the wrist.

The blade passed through. No resistance. No damage. The Nameless wasn't fully physical yet. It was close — so close — but the suit wasn't complete.

"Interesting," it said, watching me stumble from the momentum of my own missed swing. "Violence. A primitive containment method."

"Works well enough," I said, switching to silver rounds. I emptied the magazine — six shots, center mass. Each bullet passed through the Nameless without effect, embedding in the wall of the fish stall behind it.

"Your tools are designed for known threats. I am not known. I am before knowing."

Sarah dropped to her knees beside the ley line intersection. The mark on her arm was spreading up her neck now, blue lines crawling across her skin like circuitry. She was muttering — fragments of the Nameless's language, the backward synthesis chant Hargrove had been using in Bay 11.

[beat]

It looked at Sarah's arm, blazing with blue light. "You understand this. You build cages of knowledge and call them laboratories. I accept your terms."

[beat]

Sarah pulled her tablet from her bag and began writing. Not text — symbols. The fractal synthesis glyphs, flowing from her fingers like water. She drew them on the screen, then pressed the tablet against the ground, directly on the ley line intersection.

The symbols transferred. Blue light erupted from the intersection, racing outward along the ground in geometric patterns. The Nameless stood at the center, arms outstretched, and the light wrapped around it — layer upon layer, constraint upon constraint, building the suit from the outside in.

Its featureless face sharpened. Gained definition. Eyes appeared — dark, deep, but human. A nose. A mouth. The proportions shifted until it looked like a man. Tall, yes. Unsettling, certainly. But contained. Limited. Small.

The light faded. The ley line calmed. The shadows stopped moving independently. Reality snapped back into focus like a rubber band.

The Nameless — no, the contained entity, the defined thing, whatever it was now — looked at its hands. Flexed its fingers. Smiled.

"I am... small," it said, and the wonder in its voice was the most human sound I'd ever heard.

Meridian's cover-up teams arrived seventeen minutes later. Gas leak explanation deployed. Witnesses' memories adjusted. Pike Place reopened the next morning with no visible damage.

Sarah and I filed a joint report that didn't mention the Nameless, the ley lines, or the synthesis matrix. We reported a contained entity — new classification pending — and recommended further study.

Hargrove was quietly reassigned. His research was absorbed into a new division that Sarah now headed. I was promoted to Senior Cleaner, which came with a pay raise and an office I never used.

That night, we sat on the roof of Meridian Tower, looking out at a city that had no idea what had almost happened.

"Protocol Midnight," Sarah said. "That's what I'm calling the synthesis framework. A new containment protocol for entities that can't be classified."

"Fitting."

She leaned against me. I didn't move. Her arm was still marked — the blue lines faded but not gone. A reminder of the bridge she'd built.

"The Nameless was right about one thing," she said quietly. "Limitation creates purpose. I spent my whole life in labs, studying things I could never touch. And then I went into the field with you, and everything became real."

"Real is my specialty."

She laughed — small, genuine, warm — and the city lights reflected in her eyes, and I thought: maybe this is what purpose feels like. Not saving the world. Not containing the impossible. Just sitting on a roof with someone who understands the weight of knowing too much.

"Same time tomorrow?" she asked.

"Same time tomorrow."
