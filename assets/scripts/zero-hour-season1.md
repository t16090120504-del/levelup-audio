## Series Overview

In a world where superheroes must register with the government and work under corporate sponsorship, powers are regulated like firearms. Registration grants legal hero status, corporate funding, and public protection — but the fine print strips heroes of constitutional rights: no jury trial, no right to privacy, no right to refuse deployment. Registered heroes are property of their sponsors. Jake Park manifested powers at twenty-three and refused to register — because he read the contract. Operating as the vigilante "Zero," he becomes the most wanted unregistered hero in the country. The government sends their top hero, Aurora — secretly Diana Park, Captain Stellar's daughter and the system's golden girl — to capture him. But the closer Aurora gets to Zero, the more she realizes the registration system she's defended her entire life is built on a lie. And the farther she falls for the man behind the mask.

## Episode 1: "Manifestation"

[narration begins]

My powers manifested on a Tuesday. Because of course they did.

I was working the night shift at a convenience store in Eastwood — the kind of neighborhood where the streetlights work half the time and the police respond in thirty minutes if you're lucky. A guy came in with a knife, screaming about money, waving the blade at my face like he'd learned armed robbery from movies.

I raised my hands. Standard procedure. Don't be a hero. Give him the cash, file the report, move on.

He lunged.

And the world went gray.

Not dark — gray. Everything except me. The shelves, the ceiling, the robber, the flickering fluorescent lights — all of it drained of color, frozen in place. I could move. Nothing else could.

The gray lasted maybe three seconds. Color flooded back. The robber stumbled, disoriented, and I took the knife from his hand while he was still blinking. Called the cops. Filed the report. Went home.

And stared at my hands for six hours.

[beat]

I spent the next two weeks reading everything I could find about superhuman manifestation. The government's registry website. Academic papers on power classification. Legal forums. Underground message boards for what they called "unregs" — people who'd manifested powers and chosen not to register.

Registration was presented as simple and beneficial. Sign the contract, join a sponsor corporation, get training and equipment and a legal hero license. The government handled your insurance, your legal protection, your public image. In return, you agreed to deploy when called, follow orders from your handler, and abide by the Hero Conduct Code.

Simple. Clean. Patriotic.

Then I read the actual contract. All two hundred and fourteen pages of it.

Clause 7, Section 3: Registered heroes waive the right to trial by jury for any conduct-related offense. Discipline is administered by a corporate tribunal.

Clause 19, Section 8: Registered heroes surrender the right to privacy in all personal communications, medical records, and financial transactions for the duration of their registration.

I read that clause three times. Mandatory power suppression. Indefinite confinement. Based on a definition of "psychological instability" written by the same corporations that profited from controlling powered individuals.

I closed the laptop and made my decision.

My name is Jake Park. I have the ability to freeze time for everything within a thirty-meter radius while I remain fully mobile. And there is no way in hell I'm signing that contract.

## Episode 2: "Zero Hour"

A woman was being mugged in the alley behind my apartment. Two men, one gun. I heard her scream through my window, pulled on a ski mask, and went downstairs.

**POWER ACTIVATION: TEMPORAL FREEZE**
**Radius: 30 meters**
**Duration: Estimated 3.2 seconds (Subject to improvement with practice)**
**Energy Cost: Moderate**

## Episode 5: "The List"

## Episode 6: "The Network"
**Duration target: ~4 minutes (~600 words)**

"And you're still willing?"

Diana looked at me. Just a glance — quick, almost invisible. But I saw it, and Thomas saw it, and the temperature in the room shifted.

"I'm willing," she said.

## Episode 7: "The Bridge"

The way she laughed — rare, genuine, surprising — when I made a joke about the absurdity of our situation. A golden-armored corporate hero and a gray-hooded vigilante, planning to break into a government facility together.

"I'm scared, Zero."

"That's not enough."

"It's more than most people have."

"The first time I used my powers," she said softly, "I was twelve. My father was watching. He smiled. He said, 'Diana, you're going to change the world.' And I believed him."

We had a facility to assault. A prison to breach. A system to expose.

And we had each other.

It wasn't enough. But it was a start.

## Episode 8: "Blacksite Meridian"
**Duration target: ~4 minutes (~600 words)**

Blacksite Meridian looked like a corporate campus from the outside. Modernist architecture, manicured lawns, a fountain in the main courtyard. It could have been a tech company headquarters or a luxury retreat.

The fences were electrified. The courtyard was mined. And beneath the corporate facade, three hundred feet underground, was a prison that held over fifty broken heroes.

Elena's intel was precise. Security rotated every four hours. The weakest gap was at 3 AM, during the shift change between night and morning teams. A ninety-second window where the automated defense systems cycled through their diagnostic reset.

Ninety seconds. My freeze could give us seven now — ten if I pushed it, but I'd need recovery time between activations. Diana's temporal resistance was our wildcard — she could operate inside my freeze, which meant we had double the effective time.

The plan was simple. Enter through the maintenance tunnel Elena identified. Reach the suppression wing before the shift change. Free the prisoners. Get out before the defense systems came back online.

Simple. Insane. Possibly suicidal.

[beat]

We went at 3 AM.

The maintenance tunnel was exactly where Elena said — a concrete corridor, two meters wide, running from a drainage access point outside the perimeter to the facility's sub-level 1. I froze the motion sensors at the entrance, and we moved.

Diana moved differently without her armor. Not weaker — focused. Every step precise, every movement economical. She was adapting to the mission: no golden light, no wings, no logo. Just a woman with energy crackling at her fingertips, ready for anything.

Sub-level 1 was empty. Research labs — medical equipment, monitoring stations, screens displaying neural activity readings from the floors below. Diana paused at one of the screens.

"Forty-seven active subjects," she read. "Neural suppression at 78% average." Her jaw tightened. "My father's signal is in Bay 12."

"We get everyone. Not just him."

"I know."

Sub-level 2 was the suppression wing. A long corridor lined with cells — not cages, but medical pods. Each pod held a person, suspended in a fluid that glowed faintly blue, connected to machines that hummed with the frequency of neural suppression.

I opened my freeze. Seven seconds. Diana and I moved through the corridor together, her energy disrupting the pod locks while I opened them manually. One by one, the pods released their occupants. Bodies fell into our arms — emaciated, unresponsive, barely breathing.

We got to Bay 12. Inside the pod was Captain Stellar.

He was old. Gaunt. The muscles that had once held the strength of a titan were wasted, and his eyes — when they opened — were foggy and confused.

"Diana?" His voice was a whisper. Barely there.

"I'm here, Dad. I'm getting you out."

He looked at me. Past the ski mask, past the gray hoodie. His foggy eyes cleared for just a moment.

"Take care of her," he said.

Then the alarms started.

## Episode 9: "Unmasked"
**Duration target: ~4 minutes (~600 words)**

Ninety seconds wasn't enough.

We'd freed thirty-one of the forty-seven prisoners before the defense systems came back online. The alarms were deafening — red lights flooding the corridors, automated turrets deploying from the walls, and the sound of armored boots descending from above.

"Evacuation compromised," Elena said through our comm link. "Helios response team incoming. Six registered heroes, Class 5 and above. ETA: four minutes."

Four minutes. We had thirty-one unconscious or semi-conscious former heroes and a facility designed to keep them prisoner. The math was bad.

Diana made a decision.

"Get them to the tunnel. All of them. I'll hold the entrance."

"Diana—"

"That's an order, Zero." She was smiling, but her eyes were serious. "You can move thirty-one people in four minutes with your freeze. I can hold six Class-5 heroes for four minutes with my power. This is what we trained for."

She was right. The tactical logic was sound. That didn't make it easier.

"Meet me at the tunnel," I said.

"Always."

[beat]

I worked. Freeze, move, release, freeze again. Ten seconds of active time, thirty seconds of recovery. I moved bodies through the tunnel like a possessed delivery service, dragging, carrying, pulling the freed heroes toward safety. My migraines built from pressure to agony to a white-hot fire behind my eyes. I pushed through it.

Behind me, the sound of battle echoed through the facility. Energy blasts. Impact. Diana's voice, raised in something between a war cry and a scream.

I got the last of the thirty-one to the tunnel entrance as the Helios team reached the suppression wing. Through the concrete, I felt the shockwaves — Diana's power at full output, a Class-7 energy manipulator cutting loose.

I ran back in.

She was holding the corridor alone. Golden energy surrounded her — not the contained, corporate-controlled glow of her Aurora armor, but something rawer. Wilder. Her hair was loose, her eyes blazing, and the Helios team was struggling.

Four of the six were down. The remaining two — a telekinetic and a brute-strength tank — were pressing her hard. She was bleeding from a cut above her eye and her breathing was ragged.

But she was smiling.

"Zero! Get out!"

I froze time. Seven seconds. In the gray silence, I moved the two standing heroes — repositioned them, disarmed them, shifted them into disadvantageous positions. When time resumed, Diana dispatched them in seconds.

Diana pulled off her ski mask. "My name is Diana Park. Daughter of Captain Stellar. And I am done being a weapon."

She broadcast it on every frequency she could access — the facility's communication system, the emergency channel, the Network's encrypted feed. Her voice went out across the country, unfiltered, unedited.

"This is Aurora. And the registration system is a lie. I have proof. I have the suppressed. And I am not going back."

## Episode 10: "Unregistered"
**Duration target: ~5 minutes (~750 words)**

"Aurora Suffers Mental Break" read the morning headlines. "Tragedy as Hero Icon Betrays Public Trust." Helios issued a statement condemning Diana's "unstable behavior" and expressing concern for her psychological well-being — the same language they used to justify suppressing heroes who didn't comply.

The suppression order went out within hours. Diana Park, formerly known as Aurora, was classified as psychologically unstable. All registered heroes were ordered to capture her on sight.

She was now what I'd been from the beginning. Unregistered. Hunted. Alone.

Except she wasn't alone.

[beat]

[beat]

She was quieter now. Not broken — never broken — but stripped down to essentials. The corporate polish was gone, and beneath it was someone stronger. Rawer. Someone who chose her battles instead of accepting them.

"My mother called," she said one night, lying on her cot, staring at the ceiling. "She said I'd been brainwashed. That unregistered heroes had corrupted me. She sounded like a Helios press release."

"Is that what you think? That I brainwashed you?"

She turned her head to look at me. "I think you showed me a contract and asked if I'd read it. That's not brainwashing. That's literacy."

I laughed. She laughed. In a converted subway tunnel, while the government hunted us and the suppresseds slowly recovered, we laughed like it was normal.

The fight wasn't over. The registration system was still in place. The suppression facilities were still operating. Diana's broadcast had opened a door, but the institutions behind the door were massive, entrenched, and well-funded.

But we had something they didn't.

We had time. Not my temporal-freeze time, though that helped. We had momentum. Public pressure was building. More registered heroes were questioning their contracts. More suppresseds were being found and freed. The Network was growing — not just in our city, but in every city where someone manifested powers and chose to read the fine print.

Diana stood up and crossed the room to my cot. She sat beside me, her hand finding mine in the dark.

"Zero," she said.

"Jake."

"What?"

"My name is Diana. I think you should use it. We're past code names."

"Diana." The name felt different in my mouth. Real. "What do we do now?"

She leaned her head against mine. Outside, in the city above us, the world was waking up. Helios was spinning damage control. The DHR was updating my threat assessment from "moderate" to "extreme." Somewhere in Nevada, the remaining suppresseds waited in their pods, dreaming of freedom.

"What we've always done," Diana said. "We show up when things get bad. We fix them. And we don't stop until the system that created them is gone."

I squeezed her hand. "Sounds like a plan."

The gray was waiting for me. The freeze, the power, the three seconds that could change everything. But for the first time since my manifestation, I wasn't using it alone.

She was my partner. Not my handler. Not my sponsor. My partner.

And together, we were going to burn the system to the ground.
